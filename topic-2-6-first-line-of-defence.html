The three barrier types

First line of defence

Physical barriers work structurally — they block, trap, or expel pathogens using the body's own anatomy and reflexes.

BarrierHow it protects
Skin The stratum corneum consists of dead, continuously shed cells — removing adhered pathogens. Low moisture makes the surface hostile to microorganisms, which need water to survive and reproduce.
Mucous membranes Goblet cells secrete sticky mucus that physically traps pathogens. Cilia beat in coordinated waves to sweep mucus up to the throat, where it's swallowed or expelled by coughing.
Hair Nasal hairs filter large airborne particles; eyelashes protect eyes from foreign bodies. Body hair also traps sweat and sebum, which carry antimicrobial properties.
Sneezing A forceful reflex expulsion of air from the lungs clears irritants and pathogens from nasal passages, preventing them from travelling deeper into the respiratory system.
Blinking & tears Blinking spreads a tear film that continuously washes pathogens and debris from the eye surface, flushing them away mechanically.
Urine Regular urination creates a flushing action that physically expels microorganisms from the urinary tract, preventing their buildup and reducing infection risk.

Chemical barriers neutralise or destroy pathogens using enzymes, acids, and antimicrobial proteins — often working alongside physical barriers.

BarrierHow it protects
Lysozymes Antimicrobial enzymes that kill bacteria by breaking down their cell walls. Found in tears, saliva, mucus, sweat, and breast milk.
Mucus (chemical) Beyond physical trapping, mucus contains lysozymes and antibodies that chemically neutralise or destroy pathogens before they can contact epithelial surfaces.
Stomach acid Hydrochloric acid creates a pH of 1–3 that denatures proteins and disrupts pathogen membranes, killing the vast majority of ingested microorganisms.
Sweat Contains dermcidin (disrupts bacterial membranes), lysozymes, and salts that dehydrate bacteria via osmotic stress. Its slightly acidic pH (4–6) further inhibits microbial growth.
Sebum Oily secretion from sebaceous glands. Lipids (triglycerides) are broken down by beneficial bacteria into acidic free fatty acids, creating an acidic skin surface hostile to pathogens. Also contains antimicrobial proteins.
Earwax Sticky texture traps debris. Sebum contributes acidity; cerumen from ceruminous glands provides lysozymes and antimicrobial proteins that actively kill bacteria in the ear canal.

Biological barriers are living — communities of beneficial microorganisms that outcompete pathogens for space, nutrients, and resources.

Competitive exclusion principle

When two species compete for the same resources, one will eventually outcompete the other. The native microbiome exploits this — beneficial bacteria colonise skin, gut, and mucosal surfaces first, leaving pathogens unable to establish a foothold.

🦠

Native microbiome (normal flora)

Harmless bacteria on skin, in the gut, mouth, nose, and urogenital openings compete directly with pathogens for space and nutrients. Their established presence alone suppresses colonisation by harmful microorganisms.

💧

Sebum metabolism on skin

Beneficial skin bacteria metabolise triglycerides in sebum more efficiently than pathogens can. This competitive nutrient advantage lets them proliferate faster and dominate the environment, maintaining healthy microbial balance.

🛡️

Antimicrobial protein production

Some beneficial microbes actively produce antimicrobial proteins that inhibit or kill harmful bacteria, adding a biochemical dimension on top of simple resource competition.

🔑

The key principle

The most effective defence against infection is preventing pathogen entry altogether. Maintaining intact barriers and practising good hygiene supports all three barrier types simultaneously — physical, chemical, and biological — giving the immune system the best chance of never needing to fire its heavier weapons.
