2.7  ·  Second Line of Defence

When pathogens
breach the surface

Once a pathogen penetrates the first line, the innate immune system fires a rapid, non-specific internal response — the same reaction, regardless of the invader. This is the second line of defence.

How the second line works

Non-specific · Innate
🎯

Non-self recognition

Innate immune cells use pattern recognition receptors to detect pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) — surface structures found on pathogens but not on host cells. They cannot distinguish between different pathogen types, only detect "non-self."

⚡

Rapid but non-specific

Unlike the adaptive immune system, the innate response does not change or adapt over a lifetime. It responds the same way to all pathogens — fast and broad. Humans are born with it; so are bacteria, fungi, plants, and invertebrates.

Cells of the innate immune system

Leukocytes

White blood cells (leukocytes) form in the bone marrow and patrol via the circulatory system, migrating to infected tissues during an immune response. The cells below are the key players in the non-specific innate response.

Cell typeRole in the innate immune response
Monocytes Circulate in the blood and migrate to infected tissues, where they can differentiate into macrophages and dendritic cells in response to inflammation signals.
Neutrophils The most abundant white blood cell — typically the first to arrive at a site of infection. Phagocytic cells that engulf and destroy invading pathogens. Short-lived; their accumulation forms pus. Not involved in antigen presentation.
Mast cells Abundant in tissues. In response to damage or immune signals they release histamine, dilating blood vessels and increasing permeability to initiate inflammation.
Macrophages The most efficient phagocytic cell — develop from monocytes and can engulf large numbers of pathogens. Release cytokines to recruit other immune cells. Also act as antigen presenting cells, bridging innate and adaptive immunity.
Dendritic cells Found in tissue fluid; also develop from monocytes. Phagocytic, but primarily known as antigen presenting cells that activate the adaptive immune system.
Natural killer cells Lymphocytes, but part of the innate (not adaptive) immune system. Destroy cancerous or virus-infected host cells to stop spread of infection — a non-specific role analogous to cytotoxic T-cells.

Phagocytosis

Key process

What is phagocytosis?

Phagocytosis (endocytosis) is the process by which phagocytic cells — neutrophils, macrophages, and dendritic cells — recognise, engulf, and destroy pathogens. The three cell types can reside permanently in tissues, circulate the body, or be recruited to infection sites via cytokine signals.

1

Recognition

The phagocyte recognises non-self surface molecules (PAMPs) on the pathogen using pattern recognition receptors.

2

Binding & engulfment

The phagocyte binds to the pathogen and engulfs it, forming a membrane-bound sac called a phagosome inside the cell.

3

Lysosome fusion

Lysosomes (containing digestive enzymes) fuse with the phagosome, forming a phagolysosome.

4

Destruction

The digestive enzymes within the phagolysosome break down and destroy the trapped pathogen completely.

Chemical signals of the innate system

Signalling molecules
Cytokines

Cytokines (overview)

Signalling proteins secreted by leukocytes and infected cells. They regulate inflammation and immune cell communication. Over 40 types exist, including chemokines, interferons, and interleukins.

Chemokines

Chemokines

A type of cytokine released in response to infection. They function as chemical signals that guide leukocytes along concentration gradients toward the site of infection.

Interferons

Interferons

Proteins secreted by virus-infected host cells. They "interfere" with viral spread by:

  • Signalling uninfected cells to destroy RNA and reduce protein synthesis
  • Triggering programmed cell death in infected cells
  • Activating macrophages and other immune cells
Interleukins

Interleukins

Cytokines that direct immune cells to divide and differentiate. Their name reflects their role: inter (between) + leukins (leukocytes). They also help bridge the innate and adaptive immune responses via antigen presenting cells.

Histamine

Histamine

Released by mast cells (and other immune cells) in response to injury, inflammation, or allergic reactions. Causes vasodilation — widening capillaries and making them more permeable — which allows leukocytes and other immune substances to rapidly migrate from the blood into the site of infection. Unlike cytokines, histamine is not classified as a cytokine.

The complement system

Innate · Bridges adaptive

What is the complement system?

An ancient system (evolved ~700 million years ago) of 30+ antimicrobial proteins synthesised primarily by the liver. They circulate inactive in blood plasma and tissue fluid until triggered by infection or tissue damage. Upon activation, complement proteins coat pathogens in a cascade effect — each binding triggering the next — leading to three key actions.

1

Attract phagocytes (opsonisation)

Complement proteins bind to pathogen surface molecules, flagging them for destruction and drawing phagocytes to the site of infection.

2

Puncture cell membranes

Complement proteins assemble into a membrane attack complex that punches holes in the pathogen's cell membrane, causing it to rupture and die.

3

Stimulate histamine release

Complement proteins bind to mast cells, triggering histamine release — intensifying the inflammatory response and recruiting more leukocytes.

Why "complement"?

The system is named because it complements both innate and adaptive immunity. It is particularly attracted to pathogens already tagged by antibodies from the adaptive immune system — the antibodies provide attachment sites for complement proteins.

The inflammatory response

Immediate reaction

The inflammatory response is the body's immediate reaction to infection or tissue injury. Damaged or immune cells detect harmful stimuli and release cytokines and histamines, triggering a cascade that localises the response and recruits immune cells.

🔴

Redness

Increased blood flow delivers oxygen and immune cells to the area

🌡️

Heat

Also due to increased blood flow — warming the local tissue

💧

Swelling

Fluid leaks into tissues as blood vessel permeability increases

⚡

Pain

Pressure from swelling and pain-inducing chemicals like prostaglandins

StageWhat happens
Detection Damaged cells or immune cells detect pathogens or tissue injury and release cytokines and histamines.
Vasodilation Blood vessels widen (vasodilation) and become more permeable, allowing immune cells and fluid to move into the affected area.
Recruitment Chemokines guide neutrophils and macrophages to the site. These cells destroy pathogens via phagocytosis.
Repair Immune cells release blood clotting factors that seal wounds, forming a scab. Dead neutrophils accumulate as pus beneath developing scabs.

Fever

Systemic response
37°C
Normal body temperature

The standard internal temperature maintained by healthy humans at rest.

>38°C
Fever threshold

Above this, signals from the hypothalamus elevate body temperature in response to a detected infection.

Why fever helps

Elevated temperature inhibits the growth of many bacteria and viruses, accelerates immune cell activity, and improves the production of infection-fighting molecules like antibodies. Shivering and vasoconstriction raise the temperature; sweating and vasodilation bring it back down when the fever breaks.

Innate immunity across organisms

Comparative biology

Vertebrates have both innate and adaptive immunity. Fungi, invertebrates, plants, and bacteria rely solely on innate responses — they have no adaptive immune system.

Animals without a backbone — insects, arachnids, molluscs, crustaceans. Their innate immunity is their only immunity.

🦀

Exoskeleton

A tough physical barrier made of chitin prevents pathogen entry — equivalent to the first line of defence in vertebrates.

🩸

Hemocytes

Immune cells in hemolymph (the blood equivalent). They perform phagocytosis to engulf and destroy pathogens and release signalling molecules to coordinate the immune response.

🔍

Pattern recognition

Use the same principle as vertebrates — pattern recognition molecules detect non-self antigens and activate immune defences.

⚗️

Antimicrobial chemicals

Secrete lysozymes and antimicrobial peptides to neutralise or kill invading microbes.

Multicellular photosynthetic organisms — trees, grasses, flowering plants. Immobile, so innate defences are critical.

🌿

Physical barriers

Waxy cuticle, cell walls, and bark prevent pathogen entry. Some plants also practice leaf dropping — shedding infected leaves to isolate and remove infection.

🔍

Pattern recognition

Plants use pattern recognition molecules to detect pathogen-associated patterns, triggering defence responses — the same principle as vertebrate innate immunity.

☕

Antimicrobial chemicals — harvested by humans

Plants produce toxic chemicals to defend against bacteria, fungi, and insects. Humans have adapted many of these for their own use:

☕ Caffeine — stimulant 🍫 Cocoa — food manufacturing 🚬 Nicotine — insecticide / drug 💊 Quinine — antimalarial / tonic water 🌼 Pyrethrins — plant-based insecticide

Fungi defend against competing microorganisms using physical and chemical innate strategies.

🧱

Rigid cell walls

Fungal cell walls are composed of chitin — the same tough material as invertebrate exoskeletons. This provides a strong physical barrier against pathogen entry.

💊

Antimicrobial chemicals — antibiotics

Fungi produce antibiotics to inhibit or kill competing microorganisms. The most famous example is Penicillium, which naturally produces penicillin as part of its own innate defence system — a discovery that revolutionised human medicine.

Bacteria face constant attack from bacteriophages (viruses) and competing bacteria, and have evolved sophisticated innate defences.

Antibiotics

Bacteria like Streptomyces produce a wide range of antibiotics (e.g. streptomycin, tetracycline) to inhibit protein synthesis in competing bacteria. Biofilms can protect them from antibiotics produced by rivals, as the chemicals often cannot penetrate the film.

Restriction enzymes

Molecular "scissors" that recognise and cut DNA at specific restriction sites. When a virus infects a bacterium, the viral DNA is recognised as foreign and cut, preventing replication. A classic non-specific innate defence.

CRISPR — an adaptive component

CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) is a unique bacterial defence that stores fragments of past viral DNA as memory sequences. If the same virus attacks again, Cas proteins are directed to the matching viral DNA to cut and disable it. This gives CRISPR a memory component — making it the only bacterial defence with an adaptive character. Both restriction enzymes and CRISPR are now used by humans in genetic engineering.

🔑

The key principle

The second line of defence is rapid but non-specific — the innate system responds identically to all pathogens. Inflammation, fever, phagocytosis, and the complement system work together to slow and neutralise invaders. Crucially, macrophages and dendritic cells also act as antigen presenting cells, handing information to the adaptive immune system and bridging the second and third lines of defence.
