🦠 Types of Pathogens
Know the differences, sizes, and how each is treated — this comes up in MCQs and short answers.

What is a Pathogen?

A pathogen is any disease-causing microorganism. The main types you need to know are: bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, and visible parasites.

Pathogen Size (smallest → largest)

A key MCQ question! Order from smallest to largest:

Virus → Bacterium → Protozoan → Visible Parasite

Each Pathogen — What You Must Know

Infectious vs Non-Infectious Disease

  • Infectious — caused by a pathogen, can spread person to person (e.g. Influenza, Malaria, Chlamydia)
  • Non-Infectious — NOT caused by a pathogen, cannot spread (e.g. Cancer, Emphysema, Allergies, Type 2 Diabetes)

What is a Disease?

A disease is a condition that disrupts the normal functioning of the body. It has a specific set of signs and symptoms, and may be caused by pathogens, genetics, environment, or lifestyle factors.

🛡️ The Immune System
Three lines of defence — know each level, what's in it, and innate vs adaptive. Very high exam value.

Three Lines of Defence

LineTypeComponents
1st Line Innate (physical & chemical barriers) Skin, mucus, cilia, stomach acid, sweat & sebum oil, tears, coughing/sneezing, eyelashes
2nd Line Innate (internal response) Phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages), inflammation, fever
3rd Line Adaptive (specific) Antibodies, B cells, T cells (Killer T cells), memory B cells & memory T cells

⚠️ The correct 3rd line answer in the exam is C: Stomach acid → Inflammation → Antibodies

Innate vs Adaptive Immune System

  • Innate — always working, non-specific (attacks any pathogen), no preparation needed. Includes 1st and 2nd lines of defence.
  • Adaptive — needs to be "primed" before working fully. Specific — targets a particular pathogen. Includes 3rd line. Creates memory cells.

Fever — Why It Happens

Fever is an innate immune response. When pathogens invade, the body raises its temperature. This:

  • Speeds up the immune response (more phagocytes produced faster)
  • Kills or inhibits many pathogens (they struggle to survive at higher temps)

Phagocytes — How They Work

Phagocytes (neutrophils and macrophages) are white blood cells that engulf and destroy bacteria. This process is called phagocytosis. They are part of the 2nd line of defence.

They do NOT produce antibodies — that's the job of B cells (plasma cells) in the 3rd line.

Inflammation

The body responds to infection by becoming red, swollen, and painful — this is inflammation. It is part of the 2nd line of defence (innate immune response).

Vaccines & How They Work

Vaccines work by exposing the immune system to a weakened or killed pathogen (or its antigens). This "primes" the adaptive immune system so it creates memory cells. When the real pathogen appears, the immune system responds quickly.

Vaccines effectively increase the number of antibodies available against a specific pathogen.

Herd Immunity

  • Protects a whole population when enough individuals are immune to a specific pathogen
  • Can be achieved through natural infection or vaccination
  • Relates to a specific pathogen only
  • ⚠️ It does NOT only protect those exposed — it protects the whole population (including those who can't be vaccinated)

Physical vs Chemical Barriers (Innate)

  • Physical: Skin (blocks entry), mucus membranes, cilia (sweep pathogens out), eyelashes
  • Chemical: Stomach acid (kills ingested pathogens), sebum/sweat (antimicrobial), tears (contain lysozyme)
🔬 Bacteria & Lab Skills
These topics come straight from your study helpers — high chance of appearing in short answer or the experiment question.

Zone of Inhibition

When antibiotic discs are placed on an agar plate with bacteria, a clear ring may appear around a disc. This is the zone of inhibition (also called a clear zone).

  • The clear zone = bacteria cannot survive in that area → the antibiotic is effective
  • No clear zone = bacteria grow right up to the disc → the antibiotic is NOT effective against that species
  • A larger zone of inhibition = more effective antibiotic
  • The blank disc (Disc B in your exam) = control; should have no zone

In the experiment: Independent variable = the type of antibiotic disc. Variables kept constant = temperature, incubation time, amount of bacteria spread, agar type.

Control Agar Plate — Purpose

The control plate has only the bacteria with no antibiotic discs. Its purpose is to confirm that:

  • Bacteria grow normally without antibiotics (confirms bacteria are alive and growing)
  • Any zones of inhibition on the experimental plate are caused by the antibiotic, not another factor

Without the control, you can't be sure your results are valid.

Biofilms (Bacteria Defence)

A biofilm is a community of bacteria that form a protective sticky layer around themselves (like a shield).

  • Makes bacteria much harder to kill with antibiotics
  • Helps bacteria stick to surfaces (e.g. medical implants, teeth — plaque is a biofilm!)
  • Protects bacteria from the immune system
  • This is one reason Influenza/bacteria persist despite treatment

Pili (on Bacteria)

Pili (singular: pilus) are thin, hair-like projections found on the surface of bacteria. They have two main functions:

  • Attachment — allow bacteria to stick to host cells (e.g. intestine, urinary tract wall), starting infection
  • Conjugation — allow bacteria to transfer genetic material (including antibiotic resistance genes) to other bacteria

Think of pili as bacterial "grappling hooks" — they latch on and can also share DNA.

Antibiotic Resistance — How It Emerges

When antibiotics are overused or misused:

  • Most bacteria are killed, but a few with random mutations survive
  • These resistant bacteria reproduce rapidly (natural selection)
  • Resistance genes can be passed via pili (conjugation) to other bacteria
  • Result: a resistant strain takes over — antibiotics no longer work

Chlamydia example: easily treated but persists because many people don't know they're infected (asymptomatic) and don't seek treatment.

🌍 Disease Spread & Control
Transmission types, Malaria, epidemics vs pandemics — expect these in short answer and SHE sections.

Epidemic vs Pandemic

  • Epidemic — a disease outbreak that spreads rapidly within a specific region or community
  • Pandemic — a disease outbreak that spreads across multiple countries or worldwide (e.g. COVID-19 was a pandemic)

Malaria — Key Facts

  • Caused by a protozoan parasite (not a bacterium or virus!)
  • Transmitted by Anopheles mosquito (vector transmission)
  • Symptoms include cycles of high fever
  • Difficult to control because: mosquitoes are widespread, resistance to drugs/insecticides develops, limited healthcare in 3rd world countries
  • Least effective control: isolating infected individuals (it's vector-borne — the mosquito spreads it, not direct person-to-person)
  • Case numbers may be underestimated because: many 3rd world countries lack diagnostic resources/healthcare access

Influenza & COVID-19

  • Influenza can't be eradicated mainly because it mutates rapidly → old antibodies/vaccines become less effective against new strains
  • COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2): people infected by the original strain are not necessarily immune to new strains because mutations change the surface antigens — the immune system's memory cells don't recognise the new strain
  • Note: Influenza is NOT treated with antibiotics (it's a virus)

Reducing Disease Transmission — Examples

  • Vaccination programs — build herd immunity
  • Quarantine — prevents infected individuals from spreading disease (advantage); but costly and limits freedom (disadvantage)
  • Hygiene practices — handwashing, masks
  • Insecticide spraying / netting — for vector-borne diseases like Malaria
  • Safe sex practices / condoms — STIs
  • Safe food handling — food-borne illness

Developed vs Developing Countries & Disease

Infectious diseases cause ~5% of deaths in developed countries vs ~50% in developing countries. Reasons include:

  • Developed countries have better healthcare access, sanitation, clean water, and vaccination programs
  • Developing countries often lack these resources
  • International travel can spread diseases globally — a risk for both developed and developing countries
