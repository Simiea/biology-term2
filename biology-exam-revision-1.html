Q22Short Answer
What is a disease?
Model Answer
A disease is a condition that disrupts the normal functioning of an organism's body, causing specific signs or symptoms. Diseases can be infectious (caused by pathogens — bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites) or non-infectious (caused by genetic, environmental, or lifestyle factors).
Q23Short Answer
What factors could decrease the transmission of an infectious disease in a particular population? List at least three examples with explanations.
Model Answer
1. Vaccination: Creating herd immunity reduces the number of susceptible hosts, limiting transmission pathways.
2. Quarantine/isolation: Separating infected individuals prevents them from transmitting the pathogen to others.
3. Hygiene (e.g. handwashing, PPE): Reduces direct contact transmission of pathogens.
4. Vector control: Eliminating disease vectors (e.g. mosquitoes via insecticides) prevents vector-borne transmission.
5. Public health education: Informs communities on safe practices (e.g. safe sex, food hygiene) to reduce transmission.
Q24Short Answer
List two ways that pathogens enter the human body and give an example of each.
Model Answer
1. Inhalation (respiratory tract): Pathogens enter via inhaled droplets — e.g. Influenza virus is inhaled from an infected person's cough or sneeze.
2. Breaks in the skin: Cuts or wounds bypass the physical skin barrier — e.g. Staphylococcus aureus bacteria can enter through wounds and cause skin infections.
Malaria context (Q25–29): Nearly half of the global population are at risk of contracting Malaria. In 2015, there were ~214 million cases and ~438,000 deaths, most prominently in developing countries. Malaria is a protozoan parasite transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes.
Q25Short Answer
Explain one reason why the estimation of 214 million Malaria cases may be significantly below the actual number.
Model Answer
In many developing countries where Malaria is prevalent, there is limited access to healthcare facilities and diagnostic testing. Many infected individuals do not seek or cannot access medical care, meaning their cases are never diagnosed or recorded in official statistics — leading to a significant undercount.
Q26Short Answer
Suggest one reason why Malaria is difficult to control.
Model Answer
Malaria is vector-borne, meaning controlling it requires eliminating or reducing Anopheles mosquito populations across large tropical environments — not just treating the Plasmodium parasite itself. This is logistically and economically extremely challenging, particularly in resource-limited regions.
Q27Short Answer
A person infected with Malaria experiences cycles of high fever. State whether fever is an innate or adaptive immune response.
Model Answer
Fever is an innate (non-specific) immune response — part of the second line of defence. It occurs the same way regardless of which pathogen caused the infection and does not require prior exposure or memory.
Q28Short Answer
Explain why fever occurs in response to infection.
Model Answer
When infection is detected, immune cells release pyrogens (signalling molecules) that travel to the hypothalamus in the brain. The hypothalamus raises the body's temperature set-point above 37°C. The elevated temperature (above 38°C) inhibits the growth and reproduction of many pathogens, accelerates immune cell activity, and improves production of infection-fighting molecules — enhancing the body's ability to eliminate the pathogen.
Q29Short Answer
Explain how phagocytic cells aid in the immune response.
Model Answer
Phagocytic cells (neutrophils, macrophages, dendritic cells) use pattern recognition receptors to detect non-self PAMPs on pathogens. They engulf the pathogen, forming a phagosome. Lysosomes fuse with the phagosome and release digestive enzymes that break down and destroy the pathogen. Macrophages and dendritic cells also function as antigen-presenting cells (APCs) — they display pathogen fragments on MHC Class II proteins to T-helper cells, activating the adaptive immune response.
Q30Short Answer
Name three common methods by which pathogenic organisms can be transmitted to an individual, and list two pathogens for each method.
Model Answer
1. Airborne: Influenza virus; Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB).
2. Direct contact / sexually transmitted: HIV; Chlamydia trachomatis.
3. Vector-borne: Plasmodium (Malaria protozoan, via Anopheles mosquito); Dengue virus (via Aedes mosquito).

Section 3 — Antibiotic Experiment

Questions 31–34
Experiment context: Microbe A was spread onto two agar plates. On the experimental plate, four discs were placed: discs A, C and P (different antibiotics) and disc B (blank control). The second plate had only Microbe A (no discs) — this was the control. Both plates were incubated for 36 hours. Results showed clear circles around two of the four discs.
Q31Short Answer
State what the independent variable of this experiment is.
Model Answer
The independent variable is the type of antibiotic disc used (discs A, B/blank, C, and P — each a different antibiotic or no antibiotic).
Q32Short Answer
List three examples of variables that would be kept constant.
Model Answer
1. Incubation temperature — same conditions for both plates.
2. Incubation time — both plates incubated for exactly 36 hours.
3. Volume/concentration of Microbe A spread on each plate — ensures results are comparable.
(Also valid: size of antibiotic discs, type/brand of agar, volume of agar.)
Q33Short Answer
Two discs have a clear circle around them and the other two do not. What is the scientific name given to the clear circles, and what does the clear circle represent?
Model Answer
The clear circles are called zones of inhibition. They represent the area around the antibiotic disc where bacterial growth has been prevented (inhibited) by the antibiotic diffusing outward. A zone of inhibition indicates the antibiotic is effective against Microbe A — a larger zone indicates greater effectiveness.
Q34Short Answer
What is the purpose of the control agar plate?
Model Answer
The control plate (Microbe A, no antibiotic discs) serves as a baseline comparison. It confirms that Microbe A grows normally under the experimental conditions and that any zones of inhibition observed on the experimental plate are specifically caused by the antibiotics — not by environmental factors or the placement of the discs themselves.

Section 4 — SARS-CoV-2 & Immune Defence

Questions 35–38
Q35Short Answer
Explain the difference between an epidemic and a pandemic.
Model Answer
An epidemic is a rapid spread of an infectious disease within a specific region or community, above what is normally expected. A pandemic is an epidemic that has spread across multiple countries or continents — typically worldwide. COVID-19 was classified as a pandemic when SARS-CoV-2 spread globally across multiple continents in early 2020.
Q36Short Answer
Explain why individuals who survived infection with the original SARS-CoV-2 strain have not necessarily been immune to future strains.
Model Answer
The adaptive immune system produces memory B and T cells that are specific to the antigens of the original strain. When SARS-CoV-2 mutates (antigenic variation), new strains display altered surface antigens. Existing memory cells may not recognise the altered antigens, so the faster secondary immune response is not triggered. The immune system may treat the new strain as a novel pathogen, requiring a slower primary response — allowing infection to occur.
Q37Short Answer
Describe the difference between innate and adaptive immune defence mechanisms.
Model Answer
The innate (non-specific) immune system responds the same way to all pathogens — it does not change or adapt during a lifetime. It includes physical barriers (skin, mucus), chemical barriers (lysozymes, stomach acid), and internal responses such as inflammation, fever, and phagocytosis. Responses are rapid but general.

The adaptive (specific) immune system produces a targeted response to each unique antigen, involving B cells (antibody production) and T cells (destroying infected cells). It develops immunological memory, enabling a faster and stronger secondary response. It takes longer to activate initially but provides long-term, pathogen-specific immunity.
Q38Short Answer
Describe two physical and two chemical barriers the human body uses as innate protection against invading pathogens.
Model Answer
Physical barriers:
1. Skin: The dry, tough stratum corneum is an impassable physical barrier; dead cells are continually shed, removing adhered pathogens.
2. Mucous membranes & cilia: Sticky mucus traps inhaled pathogens; cilia beat in coordinated waves to sweep mucus and trapped pathogens to the throat to be expelled.

Chemical barriers:
1. Stomach acid (HCl): pH 1–3 denatures proteins and destroys most ingested pathogens.
2. Lysozymes: Enzymes in tears, saliva, and mucus that break down bacterial cell walls, killing them on contact.

Q39a–dComprehension
Chlamydia is a sexually transmitted infection caused by Chlamydia trachomatis. Questions refer to Australian diagnosis trends (2002–2011).
Q39a. Describe the trend in the chlamydia diagnosis rate in Australia between 2002 and 2011.
Model Answer
The chlamydia diagnosis rate in Australia showed a consistent, significant upward trend between 2002 and 2011 among young Australians, with the rate increasing steadily each year across the period.
Q39b. State whether males or females are more likely to be diagnosed with chlamydia.
Model Answer
Females are more likely to be diagnosed with chlamydia, possibly because females are more routinely screened (e.g. through regular gynaecological check-ups), resulting in a higher detection rate.
Q39c. Suggest one reason for the increase in diagnosis rate between 2002 and 2011.
Model Answer
Increased availability and accessibility of testing (e.g. online testing, GP screening campaigns) means more people are being tested, resulting in more diagnoses — even if actual infection rates did not increase proportionally.
Q39d. Chlamydia is easily treated with antibiotics. Suggest a reason why the pathogen persists in the population despite easy treatment.
Model Answer
Chlamydia is often asymptomatic — many infected individuals have no symptoms and are unaware they are infected. Without symptoms, they do not seek testing or treatment, and continue transmitting the pathogen to new sexual partners, sustaining its presence in the population.
Q40Comprehension
Explain how the emergence of a resistant strain of bacteria might be the result of an overuse of antibiotics.
Model Answer
In any bacterial population, random mutations occur naturally — some of which may confer resistance to antibiotics. When antibiotics are overused or misused (e.g. prescribed unnecessarily, not completing courses), susceptible bacteria are killed, but resistant individuals survive and reproduce — a process of natural selection. Resistant bacteria pass on their resistance genes to offspring and can transfer them to other bacteria via horizontal gene transfer (e.g. plasmids). Over time, the resistant strain becomes dominant. The result is a resistant strain (e.g. MRSA) that cannot be effectively treated by conventional antibiotics.
Global health context (Q41–43): Infectious diseases account for ~5% of deaths in developed countries but ~50% in developing countries. More than 50 million people from developed countries visit developing countries every year.
Q41SHE
Suggest two reasons why infectious disease mortality rates differ greatly between developed and developing countries.
Model Answer
1. Access to healthcare: Developed countries have better access to hospitals, vaccines, antibiotics, and medical treatments — reducing deaths from infectious diseases.
2. Sanitation and clean water infrastructure: Developing countries may lack adequate sewage treatment and safe drinking water, increasing exposure to waterborne and faecal-oral pathogens and promoting disease spread.
Q42SHE
State one consequence of international travel to the health of the global population. Then state three precautions travellers can take to prevent travel-related infectious disease.
Model Answer
Consequence: International travel can rapidly spread infectious diseases across borders, accelerating the conversion of localised outbreaks into epidemics or pandemics (as seen with COVID-19 in 2020).

Precautions:
1. Receive destination-specific vaccinations before travelling (e.g. Yellow Fever, Hepatitis A).
2. Use insect repellent and wear protective clothing to prevent vector-borne infections (e.g. Malaria, Dengue).
3. Drink only bottled or boiled water and consume only safely prepared food to avoid waterborne pathogens.
Q43SHE
State one advantage and one disadvantage of quarantining returning travellers.
Model Answer
Advantage: Quarantine prevents potentially infected travellers from transmitting a disease to the local population during the incubation period, reducing the risk of an outbreak or epidemic.

Disadvantage: Quarantine is economically costly (requiring accommodation and monitoring) and imposes personal hardship and social disruption on travellers, which may discourage international travel and impact tourism and trade.
