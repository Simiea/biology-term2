MHC proteins — identifying self vs non-self

Key mechanism

Major histocompatibility complex (MHC) proteins are cell-surface markers that present antigen fragments for specific recognition by T cells — unlike the general pattern recognition of the innate system.

MHC Class I

On nearly all nucleated cells

Presents peptide fragments from intracellular proteins. Healthy cells display normal self peptides. Infected or abnormal cells display foreign/altered peptides — signalling cytotoxic T cells to destroy them.

MHC Class II

On antigen-presenting cells only

Expressed primarily on APCs (dendritic cells, macrophages). Presents peptides from extracellular pathogens to T-helper cells, activating the full adaptive immune response.

Antigen-presenting cells (APCs) — bridging innate and adaptive

Dendritic cells and macrophages detect, engulf, and digest pathogens as part of the innate response. They then display digested antigen fragments on their surface via MHC Class II proteins. T-helper cells recognise this displayed antigen and are activated — triggering the full adaptive immune cascade.

The lymphatic system

Infrastructure

A network of vessels, nodes, and specialised organs that circulates lymph — a fluid containing lymphocytes — and filters it to remove pathogens, cellular debris, and foreign particles.

Organ / structureRole
Bone marrowPrimary lymphoid organ. Site where all leukocytes (including B cells and T cells) originate from stem cells. B cells mature here.
Thymus glandPrimary lymphoid organ. T cells migrate here from the bone marrow to mature and become immunocompetent.
Lymph nodesSecondary lymphoid organs scattered throughout the body. Filter lymph and house immune cells — sites for activating lymphocytes when antigens are detected.
SpleenFilters blood, removes old red blood cells, and provides a site for immune cell activation and T-lymphocyte contact.
Tonsils & adenoidsTrap pathogens entering through the mouth and nose, providing a first contact point for immune cells in the respiratory tract.

T lymphocytes & cell-mediated immunity

Cell-mediated response

How the cell-mediated response begins

Every individual has billions of unique T-helper cells, each capable of binding to a different specific antigen. This means there is theoretically a T-helper cell for every possible pathogen. These cells wait in the lymphatic system until an APC presents their matching antigen via MHC Class II — then they are activated.

The four types of T cell

🤝

T-helper cells

The bridge between innate and adaptive responses. Activated by APCs presenting a specific antigen. They then activate T-cytotoxic cells and B cells by secreting cytokines, and can also migrate to the site of infection directly.

⚔️

T-cytotoxic cells (T-killer cells)

Directly kill infected, cancerous, or abnormal host cells. They recognise non-self antigen fragments presented on MHC Class I molecules and secrete chemicals that induce apoptosis — a clean, controlled cell death.

🧠

T-memory cells

Long-lived T cells that remember specific pathogens previously encountered. Upon re-exposure, they rapidly activate and mount a faster, stronger secondary immune response — the basis of long-term specific immunity.

⚖️

T-suppressor cells (regulatory T cells)

Mature in the thymus. Downregulate immune responses once the infection is cleared to prevent excessive activation and autoimmune diseases, where the immune system mistakenly attacks self cells.

Sequence of events — cell-mediated response

1

APC presents antigen

A dendritic cell or macrophage engulfs a pathogen, digests it, and presents antigen fragments on MHC Class II proteins at its surface.

2

T-helper cell activated

The APC locates the unique T-helper cell among billions in the lymphatic system with the matching receptor. This takes time — contributing to the delay of the primary response.

3

Clonal expansion

The activated T-helper cell divides rapidly into thousands of identical copies, amplifying the response.

4

Differentiation

Copies differentiate into: T-cytotoxic cells (kill infected cells), cells that migrate to the infection site, cells that secrete cytokines to activate B cells, and T-memory cells for future protection.

5

T-cytotoxic cells destroy infected cells

T-cytotoxic cells recognise non-self antigens on infected host cells (via MHC Class I) and induce apoptosis, eliminating the pathogen's replication factory.

B lymphocytes & humoral immunity

Humoral response

What is humoral immunity?

Humoral immunity targets extracellular pathogens — those found in the blood and lymph. Like T-helper cells, every individual has billions of unique B cells, each capable of recognising a different antigen. Once activated, B cells differentiate into plasma cells that secrete specific antibodies at up to 2,000 per second.

How B cells are activated

1

Antigen recognition

B cells can be activated by directly recognising specific antigens via surface antibodies, by antigens presented by T-helper cells, or by cytokines secreted by T-helper cells.

2

Clonal expansion

The activated B cell differentiates and divides until thousands of identical copies exist.

3

Plasma cell secretion

Copies differentiate into B-plasma cells that migrate to the site of infection and secrete approximately 2,000 antibodies per second into the bloodstream.

4

Memory formation

Some activated B cells become B-memory cells, persisting in the body to enable a faster, stronger secondary response upon future exposure to the same antigen.

Antibodies

Y-shaped proteins

Y-shaped proteins produced by B-plasma cells. Each antibody is specific to one antigen. They bind to antigens on pathogens, toxins, pollen, blood cell surface molecules, and proteins on transplanted tissues.

🚫

Neutralisation

Block pathogens and toxins from infecting or damaging cells.

🧪

Anti-toxin

Bind to harmful toxins, rendering them harmless before they can cause damage.

🏷️

Opsonisation

Mark pathogens for easier identification and engulfment by phagocytes.

⛓️

Immobilisation

Bind to bacterial flagella, stopping bacteria from moving and spreading.

🍇

Agglutination

Clump pathogens together, making them easier for phagocytes to engulf in bulk.

💥

Complement activation

In some cases, assist in activating the complement system, causing pore formation and pathogen lysis.

Types of immunity

Active vs passive

Immunity is classified by how it is acquired — whether the individual's own adaptive immune system generates the protection (active), or whether ready-made antibodies are received from another source (passive); and whether the route was natural or artificial.

Natural + Active

Natural active immunity

The body produces its own immune response after natural exposure to a pathogen. Memory cells develop, providing long-term protection.

e.g. Recovering from influenza — immunity develops, memory cells persist
Artificial + Active

Artificial active immunity

The body produces an immune response after exposure to antigens via vaccination. Stimulates memory cell formation without causing disease.

e.g. MMR vaccine stimulates immune response without infection
Natural + Passive

Natural passive immunity

Antibodies are transferred from one individual to another naturally — the recipient's immune system does not produce them. Short-term protection only.

e.g. Maternal antibodies passed through placenta or breast milk
Artificial + Passive

Artificial passive immunity

Pre-made antibodies are injected into a person to provide immediate but short-term protection. No memory cells are formed.

e.g. Antivenom for snake bites; rabies antibody treatment

Active vs passive — the key difference

Active immunity (natural or artificial) always involves the individual's own adaptive immune system generating memory cells — giving long-term protection. Passive immunity provides instant but temporary protection because no memory cells are formed; antibodies are eventually broken down and not replenished.

🔑

The key principle

The adaptive immune system's power lies in its specificity and memory. Billions of unique B and T cells ensure the body can respond to virtually any pathogen. The primary response is slow — but it builds memory cells. Every subsequent exposure triggers a secondary response that is faster, stronger, and more targeted — which is precisely why vaccines work.
